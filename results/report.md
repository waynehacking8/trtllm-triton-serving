## 2. FP8 vs BF16 — same model, same GPUs

| concurrency | BF16 tok/s | FP8 tok/s | FP8 speedup | BF16 ITL ms | FP8 ITL ms |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 215 | 272 | 1.26× | 4.29 | 3.33 |
| 4 | 898 | 1137 | 1.27× | 4.36 | 3.38 |
| 16 | 3291 | 4263 | 1.30× | 4.60 | 3.54 |
| 32 | 6299 | 7895 | 1.25× | 4.84 | 3.76 |
| 64 | 11474 | 13469 | 1.17× | 5.07 | 4.17 |
| 128 | 18915 | 20232 | 1.07× | 5.82 | 5.30 |

FP8 (W8A8, Hopper FP8 tensor cores) wins most at **low concurrency**, where decode is memory-bandwidth-bound: halving weight bytes cuts per-token weight traffic, so ITL drops ~20–25%. Under heavy batching the workload shifts toward compute/KV-cache pressure and the FP8 edge narrows — the honest, regime-dependent answer an SA gives instead of a single headline number.

## 3. Throughput at a tail-latency SLA

Max sustained throughput while keeping **TTFT p99** under each budget:

| stack | p99≤100ms | p99≤200ms | p99≤500ms |

This is the number that matters for capacity planning: pick the SLA, read the sustainable throughput, size the fleet.
